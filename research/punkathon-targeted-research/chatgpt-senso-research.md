## Personal finance frameworks relevant to young adults

Budgeting frameworks are **decision shortcuts** (rules-of-thumb) that help people avoid “blank-page budgeting.” For 18–30 year-olds with low-to-medium income, the best framework tends to be the one that:  
1) is easy to remember, 2) is resilient to month-to-month volatility, 3) makes saving semi-automatic, and 4) reduces the need for constant willpower (because impulsive/digital spending is the default environment). citeturn3view0turn22view2

### The four pillars approach

Because “the four pillars” is used in **multiple** ways in personal finance and investing, it’s useful to treat it as two complementary frameworks:

#### Four pillars of personal finance  
A common “four pillars” model in financial wellbeing programs is: **budgeting**, **saving**, **retirement planning**, and **wise investing**. citeturn11search4

**Core idea:** build stability first (cash-flow control + savings), then invest for long-term goals, while keeping retirement planning explicit (not implicit “someday”). citeturn11search4

**Practical example (young worker, €1,500 net/month):**  
Budgeting pillar = define a monthly spending plan and a weekly “guardrail” for variable spending (e.g., food delivery, nightlife).  
Saving pillar = set an automatic transfer (e.g., €75 on payday) to build a buffer.  
Retirement pillar = even €25–€50/month into a long-term plan introduces the habit and de-risks procrastination.  
Investing pillar = use diversified, low-complexity instruments suitable to risk tolerance (the “how” varies by country/regulation).

**Best suited for:**  
Young adults who are overwhelmed by “finance content,” but can commit to building habits progressively; also good for a bank-led education program because it maps cleanly to product categories (accounts, savings, pensions, investments). citeturn11search4turn14view0

**Strengths:**  
It is **complete** (covers both stability and growth) and easy to turn into a staged learning journey (“level 1–4”). citeturn11search4

**Limitations:**  
It can feel abstract without personal numbers; “investing” becomes intimidating if presented before the user has a buffer and debt clarity—an issue highlighted in Italy’s young-adult data (low future planning + modest market participation). citeturn22view0turn4view2

#### Four pillars of investing (Bernstein)  
William Bernstein’s well-known framing (popularized via his book *The Four Pillars of Investing*) is: **theory**, **history**, **psychology**, and the **business** of investing. citeturn11search1

**Core idea:** being a good investor is less about “tips” and more about understanding:  
- the basic mechanics of risk/return (theory),  
- how markets behaved across regimes (history),  
- how humans sabotage themselves (psychology), and  
- how the industry’s incentives can conflict with yours (business). citeturn11search1

**Practical example (first-time investor):**  
A young adult tempted by “hot” assets uses the four pillars to sanity-check:  
- Theory: “Do I understand what risk I’m taking?”  
- History: “Have similar narratives appeared before?”  
- Psychology: “Am I FOMO-buying?”  
- Business: “Who profits if I churn trades or buy expensive products?” citeturn11search1

**Best suited for:**  
Young adults already curious about investing (or exposed to “finfluencer” content), especially because Bank of Italy data shows heavy reliance on online sources and social media for economic/financial info. citeturn3view0

**Strengths:**  
It directly addresses the behavioral traps that derail real-world investing. citeturn11search1

**Limitations:**  
It is not a budgeting system by itself; it must be paired with a cash-flow framework (otherwise learning remains theoretical). citeturn11search1turn11search4

### The 65-20-15 rule compared to 50-30-20

#### The 50-30-20 rule  
**Core idea:** allocate after-tax income into three buckets: **50% needs**, **30% wants**, **20% savings/debt**—a simple, widely-repeated budgeting heuristic popularized by Elizabeth Warren and Amelia Warren Tyagi’s *All Your Worth*. citeturn11search30

**Practical example (€1,600 net/month):**  
Needs €800, Wants €480, Savings & debt payoff €320.

**Best suited for:**  
People who want a lightweight rule and don’t want to categorize every transaction; also a good “diagnostic” to see where money is going. citeturn11search30

**Strengths:**  
Very memorable; creates an explicit savings target without complex spreadsheets. citeturn11search30

**Limitations:**  
In high-cost housing areas (common in/around major cities), “needs” often exceed 50%—forcing “rule failure” that can demotivate. This matters because many young adults remain financially constrained (e.g., high share living with parents in Italy) and may already feel budgeting “doesn’t work.” citeturn3view0turn22view0

#### The 65-20-15 rule  
In recent years, a popular “tweak” is **65% essentials**, **20% fun**, **15% savings/investing**—presented as a pragmatic adaptation when essentials are structurally higher than 50%. citeturn21view0 A common popularization is via personal finance creator Nischa Shah’s “65/20/15” framing, with similar bucket meanings (essentials, fun, future self). citeturn20view1turn21view0

**Practical example (€1,600 net/month):**  
Essentials €1,040; Fun €320; Savings/investing €240.

**Best suited for:**  
Young adults early in their careers (or living in expensive housing markets) who currently cannot keep “needs” at ~50% without unrealistic deprivation. citeturn21view0turn3view0

**Strengths:**  
It “normalizes reality” (high essentials) while still protecting a savings habit. citeturn21view0

**Limitations:**  
It can underfund long-term goals if 15% is too low for the person’s situation; it also doesn’t explicitly separate emergency fund building from investing, and it can hide debt problems if “essentials” includes only minimum payments. citeturn20view1turn22view2

**How to choose between 50-30-20 and 65-20-15 (practical heuristic):**  
If you consistently break 50% “needs” **without** luxury spending, then 65-20-15 may be a more honest starting point; the goal is not to “win the rule,” but to maintain a sustainable savings habit while you work on income growth and/or fixed-cost reduction. citeturn21view0turn20view1

### Other widely-cited frameworks useful for low-to-medium income young adults

#### Zero-based budgeting  
**Core idea:** give **every euro** a job so that income minus planned outflows equals zero (including savings categories—zero does **not** mean “spend everything”). citeturn11search11

**Practical example (€1,500 net/month):**  
Rent 600, bills 120, groceries 220, transport 80, savings 100, debt extra 50, phone 15, subscriptions 25, gifts 20, “fun” 120, buffer/misc 150 → total 1,500 (nothing unassigned).

**Best suited for:**  
People who feel money “disappears” and need control; also very good when income is tight because it forces trade-offs explicitly. citeturn11search11

**Strengths:**  
High clarity; reduces end-of-month surprises; makes “small leaks” visible. citeturn11search11

**Limitations:**  
More effort; can feel rigid; requires monthly maintenance (which some young adults may not sustain without automation and coaching). citeturn11search11turn3view0

#### Pay yourself first (reverse budgeting)  
**Core idea:** automate savings/investing first; then live on what remains (sometimes called “reverse budgeting”). citeturn18search14turn18search22

**Practical example (€1,500 net/month):**  
Autotransfer €100 to savings + €25 to long-term investments on payday; bills next; the remaining money becomes the operational spending ceiling.

**Best suited for:**  
Young adults who won’t track categories, but can set up a couple of automatic transfers. This connects well to bank-led education because it’s operationally simple to implement in products. citeturn18search22turn14view0

**Strengths:**  
Low maintenance; reduces the need for willpower; builds a savings habit quickly. citeturn18search14turn18search22

**Limitations:**  
If someone has high-interest debt, “saving first” can be mathematically inferior to paying down expensive debt; also difficult if income is volatile or the person is truly paycheck-to-paycheck. citeturn18search10turn22view2

#### Envelope system (cash stuffing)  
**Core idea:** allocate spending money into envelopes (physical or digital equivalents) for variable categories; once an envelope is empty, spending stops for that category. citeturn18search5turn18search9

**Practical example:**  
A student sets envelopes for “food delivery” (€40/week), “going out” (€25/week), “clothes” (€30/month). The constraint is visible and immediate.

**Best suited for:**  
Impulse spenders and “digital-native” spenders who need friction; NerdWallet explicitly notes it can benefit impulse spenders and new budgeters. citeturn18search9

**Strengths:**  
Strong behavioral effect; tangible constraints. citeturn18search5turn18search9

**Limitations:**  
Less realistic for fully digital payments; requires discipline to enforce; doesn’t automatically solve fixed-cost issues. citeturn18search5

#### The 60% solution  
**Core idea:** keep “committed/essential spending” at around 60% of income, leaving room for goals, irregular expenses, and fun; associated with Richard Jenkins’ “60% solution” framing. citeturn18search12turn18search24

**Best suited for:**  
People who want a high-level target and don’t want detailed budgeting categories. citeturn18search24turn18search12

**Strengths:**  
Simple; focuses attention on what causes stress: high fixed costs. citeturn18search12

**Limitations:**  
60% essentials can be unattainable for some young adults in expensive housing markets; also, it’s more of a “design principle” than a full budgeting system. citeturn18search24turn3view0

#### Kakeibo (mindful budgeting journal)  
**Core idea:** use a simple journal to record income/outgoings and reflect on spending, aiming to increase mindfulness and intentionality (“household financial ledger” concept). citeturn18search3turn18search11

**Best suited for:**  
People who respond to reflection and habit-building more than spreadsheets; helpful against mindless small spending.

**Strengths:**  
Builds awareness of *why* spending happens (not just how much). citeturn18search3turn18search11

**Limitations:**  
Manual tracking burden; may not stick without a strong routine.

## Common personal finance mistakes among young adults in Italy and Europe

The most frequent mistakes cluster into:  
- **behavioral drift** (impulse spending, subscriptions, social-driven purchases),  
- **missing buffers** (weak emergency savings),  
- **short-term credit traps** (BNPL, revolving credit, installment misunderstandings), and  
- **concept gaps** (interest, compound interest, inflation, diversification).

### What recent Italian evidence says about ages 18–34

The **Bank of Italy** ran a dedicated survey on young adults **18–34** in Italy (fielded in **Q1 2023**, nearly **5,400** respondents). citeturn3view0turn2search7 Key findings directly map to “mistakes”:

Financial knowledge gaps are large. Only **35%** answered correctly the key concepts set (“inflation, interest rates, risk diversification” as the headline “big three”), and the hardest concept—**compound interest**—had only **30%** correct responses. citeturn3view0turn4view2 This aligns precisely with the common “installment plan” misunderstanding: people may grasp *monthly payment* but not *total cost* or compounding.

Budgeting and planning behaviors skew toward “today,” not “future.” Bank of Italy’s behavioral table shows: high attention to **sustainability of expenditures** (89.3%) and **meeting payment deadlines** (76.8%), but weaker **retirement planning** (48.4%) and **savings capacity** (43.3%). citeturn22view0turn4view2 In plain terms: many young adults manage current obligations, but fewer reliably build slack.

Financial product usage indicates limited long-term investing penetration. In the same survey, only **12.4%** reported a supplementary pension plan, and **13.9%** had subscribed to shares/equities/bonds at least once. citeturn4view2 This suggests that “investing is for later” remains a dominant mindset—consistent with the low retirement planning result. citeturn22view0

The “attention economy” strongly shapes where young adults learn. Over **80%** of respondents keep up with economic/financial topics mostly via **social media, TV, and websites/magazines**, and the report notes that **mobile apps are the preferred tool for learning**; also, preference for **banks and financial institutions as information sources increases with age**. citeturn3view0 This is crucial for BTM: it implies an opportunity for a bank to deliver education in the *formats young people already use*.

### EU-wide patterns relevant to ages 18–30

The European Commission’s **Flash Eurobarometer 525** (fieldwork **March–April 2023**) reports that across the EU: **18%** of citizens have **high** financial literacy, **64%** medium, and **18%** low. citeturn7search3turn8view0 The survey explicitly indicates that financial education should target **women, younger people, and lower-income/education groups** because these groups score lower on average. citeturn7search3turn8view0

Emergency savings are a visible weakness even at EU scale. Eurobarometer reports that **16%** of respondents say they have **no emergency savings**. citeturn10view1turn22view1 It also shows the resilience gap by age: respondents aged **18–24** are much less likely to say they could cover **6 months or more** of living expenses if they lost their main income source (the report mentions **17%** for ages 18–24). citeturn10view1 Lack of buffers is the practical reason “small debt” becomes an emergency.

### Modern traps: BNPL, short-term credit, subscription traps, and fraud

EU supervisors explicitly flag the rise of BNPL-like products as a consumer risk. The European Banking Authority’s **Consumer Trends Report 2024/25** describes growing consumer credit since 2020, especially **small, fast, accessible, short-term credit**, increasingly framed as **BNPL**. citeturn5view0 It highlights recurring problems: weak creditworthiness assessment and poor/late pre-contractual information. citeturn5view0turn22view2

Crucially for the 18–30 segment, the EBA notes that BNPL risks include **aggressive advertising**, easy accessibility, and targeting of **vulnerable consumers**, explicitly including **young people** and those with **low financial literacy**; it also describes “debt habituation and accumulation” from stacking multiple small credits. citeturn22view2turn6view0 This directly supports education interventions that operate **at the moment of purchase**.

EU policy is responding by bringing BNPL into stronger consumer credit protections. The European Commission explains that the **Consumer Credit Directive was revised in 2023** with broader scope that also applies to **“buy now, pay later” schemes**, and it requires Member States to establish **debt advisory services**. citeturn2search24 The legal text (Directive (EU) 2023/2225) explicitly discusses BNPL schemes in its recitals. citeturn2search5

Subscription-related harm appears in two ways: (1) “subscription creep” (too many recurring services), and (2) subscription *fraud/traps*. The EBA explicitly lists “**subscription traps**” among observed fraud techniques affecting consumers. citeturn6view0 For “subscription creep,” industry analysis suggests younger consumers are more likely to accumulate multiple video subscriptions (e.g., one Oliver Wyman analysis estimates **63% of under-35s** have **three or more** video subscriptions vs **39%** for others). citeturn7search13 Even if exact figures vary by country and category, the consistent pattern is: **youth = higher subscription density**, making “forgotten subscriptions” a realistic budget leak.

## BTM and the competitive landscape in Italy

### BTM profile and the NEXT young community

**BTM’s current footprint and positioning (from BTM’s own communications):**  
- Operates in **Torino and Cuneo** provinces. citeturn23search3  
- “Masse intermediate” exceed **€2.3B**, serving **30,000 clients**, including **10,500 members**. citeturn13view0  
- **CET1 ratio 30.6%** at **31.12.2025**; described as among the highest in the Italian banking system. citeturn13view0  
- Branch strategy emphasizes **physical presence** and relationship banking even while the broader system trends toward branch reduction; BTM frames this as “radicamento e fiducia.” citeturn13view0

BTM is a cooperative bank (Credito Cooperativo di Casalgrasso e Sant’Albano Stura) and is presented as participating in the **Cassa Centrale Banca** group structure (noted in BTM site footer and in Cassa Centrale communications about BTM). citeturn13view0turn23search6 This means that while BTM is “small” in local footprint, it may still have access to **group-level infrastructure and shared services**, an important feasibility lever.

**NEXT program:**  
BTM officially announced **Next** as its new “Young Community” dedicated to young members/clients, with an official launch event on **25 October** in **Carmagnola**. citeturn14view0turn16view0 Next’s model includes workshops/events and an **Advisory Board** of young members that proposes initiatives, offers feedback, and contributes to communication/social presence. citeturn14view0turn16view0

This aligns strongly with what Italian data says young adults want: Bank of Italy reports that (a) **mobile apps** are a preferred learning tool, and (b) reliance on social/digital channels is high—meaning a youth program can succeed if it ships education in digital-native formats and links it to real-life decisions. citeturn3view0

### Cooperative banks vs large banks: what’s genuinely different

Italian cooperative credit banks (BCCs) are characterized by **mutualistic identity**, **territorial focus**, and **member governance** (democratically elected boards, “one member, one vote” logic). A Federcasse report describes how Italian BCCs are organized into cooperative banking groups (including **Cassa Centrale** and **ICCREA**) while local banks keep their own license and member governance. citeturn12search4

At the European level, the cooperative banking model is repeatedly summarized with themes like **proximity**, **democratic governance**, and **local engagement**. citeturn12search5turn12search9 These features translate into concrete value propositions:

A small cooperative bank can often do **relationship-based customization** better: local context, faster informal feedback loops, in-person trust, and community programs that feel authentic (like NEXT’s events + advisory mechanisms). citeturn13view0turn14view0

A large bank can do scale-heavy tech and platform bets better. Two recent examples:  
- **Intesa Sanpaolo** commercially launched **Isybank** (new digital bank) in 2023 and enhanced the product catalogue afterward; it also describes a cloud-native tech architecture (“isytech”) as a major enabler of its 2026–2029 plan. citeturn19search0turn19search1  
- **UniCredit** completed the acquisition of **Aion Bank and Vodeno** to gain an “innovative, scalable, flexible cloud-based platform” based on APIs and built-in smart contract technology—illustrating how big banks can buy/build core digital banking infrastructure at scale. citeturn19search2

These “scale moves” are not realistic for a 21-branch local BCC to replicate directly—but they *are* useful signals for what small banks should **not** try to do alone.

### What initiatives are realistic for a bank of BTM’s size vs scale-only moves

**Realistic for BTM (especially as post-hackathon pilots):**  
Building “experience layers” on top of existing banking rails: education, behavior change, and community engagement overlays that don’t require replacing core banking. This matches BTM’s own stance that technology should be an enabling tool while preserving human relationship value. citeturn13view0turn14view0

Launching small, measurable experiments through NEXT: because NEXT already uses workshops and an advisory model, it can serve as a structured beta community for product discovery and iteration. citeturn14view0turn16view0

**Usually scale-only (or group-level only):**  
Building a fully new digital bank (like Isybank) or acquiring cloud core infrastructure (like Vodeno) is typically “big-bank scale,” requiring substantial capital, ops capacity, and specialized risk/compliance machinery. citeturn19search0turn19search2

**Compliance reality check for AI prototypes (why “small & safe” wins):**  
- The EU AI Act timeline indicates phased obligations: general provisions (including AI literacy) and prohibitions applied from **February 2025**, rules for general-purpose AI obligations from **August 2025**, and broader enforcement/high-risk obligations later (with “majority of rules” applying from **August 2026**). citeturn17search5  
- DORA applies from **17 January 2025**, raising the bar for ICT risk management and operational resilience in financial entities. citeturn17search12turn17search0  

For a small bank, this usually favors pilots that minimize sensitive data exposure, use strong governance, and keep the AI role focused on **education and decision support** rather than automated “financial decisions.”

## AI-powered financial education ideas for BTM

### Design constraints implied by the research

An AI education product aimed at BTM’s 18–30 audience should reflect five realities from the evidence:

Young adults often misunderstand core concepts like interest/compound interest, and future planning is weak. citeturn3view0turn22view0  
Young adults consume financial information heavily through social/digital channels; mobile apps are a preferred learning format. citeturn3view0  
Short-term credit (BNPL) is rising and can cause “small debts stacking into big debt,” with youth explicitly identified as a vulnerable target group. citeturn22view2turn2search24  
Subscription traps and fraud are real (and increasingly AI-enabled), so education must include “anti-scam” literacy and safe UX. citeturn6view0turn22view2  
BTM’s differentiator is local trust + community + human relationship; the AI experience should feel like a **mentor** embedded in a community, not a faceless chatbot. citeturn13view0turn14view0

### Divergent ideas

These are intentionally **unfiltered** (10–15 ideas), optimized for novelty and usefulness rather than feasibility.

1) **Financial “Mirror Mode”**: upload bank statements / receipts; AI produces a brutally simple “truth summary” + 3 behavior levers (top leak, top risk, top easiest win) and updates weekly.

2) **BNPL Reality Lens**: browser/mobile “purchase overlay” that converts installments into total cost, fee risk, and “future you impact” in plain language, personalized to the user’s monthly slack.

3) **Subscription Genome**: AI detects recurring payments + likely “forgotten” subscriptions, predicts cancellation friction, and generates step-by-step cancellation scripts and reminders.

4) **Impulse Interceptor (72-hour AI escrow)**: when the user wants to buy something, AI proposes a 72-hour “cooldown contract” with personalized alternatives (“swap,” “rent,” “buy used,” “wait for paycheck,” etc.) and tracks outcomes.

5) **Community Savings Battles (NEXT League)**: NEXT members join monthly challenges (“Build €100 buffer,” “No-delivery week,” “Debt-killer sprint”), with AI generating individualized missions and team scoring.

6) **SENSO-style voice coach**: a voice-first mentor that answers in the user’s own numbers and links each decision to a learning path and bank/partner actions. fileciteturn0file0

7) **Micro-simulator for life choices**: “If I move out?” “If I buy a car?” AI builds a scenario model from real spending and shows probability bands (best/base/worst).

8) **AI “Explain My Payslip”**: parse payslips; explain net vs gross, taxes, overtime, and creates a “salary upgrade” action plan.

9) **Fraud & Scam Gym**: AI generates personalized scam simulations (smishing/vishing, subscription traps, fake job offers) and trains recognition with a score, tuned to local patterns.

10) **Auto “Sinking Funds” engine**: identifies irregular annual costs (insurance, travel, gifts) and proposes a micro-saving schedule to smooth them.

11) **Emotional spending detector**: uses time + merchant patterns to flag likely emotional spending and offers alternative actions (walk, call a friend, “delayed checkout”), measuring outcomes.

12) **Financial literacy “Duolingo” with your own data**: short daily quizzes that use the user’s real spending patterns (“If your delivery spend stays at €X, what is the annual cost?”).

13) **Local deals + budget alignment**: partner with local merchants to recommend “budget-compatible” options (e.g., affordable gym, local events) instead of expensive alternatives.

14) **AI-assisted “Ask the Advisor” prep**: the app generates a one-page briefing before a branch appointment: user goals, constraints, questions to ask, trade-offs to consider.

15) **Next Advisory Board Idea Radar**: AI mines anonymized theme clusters from community conversations to help the Board propose initiatives with evidence, not vibes.

### Filtered ideas

Below is a feasibility-and-alignment filter for a **small cooperative bank** with a real chance of becoming a post-hackathon pilot through **NEXT**.

#### Ranking logic used
Feasibility is highest when the idea:  
- can run as a **standalone web app** without core banking replacement,  
- uses bank data conservatively (or starts from user-uploaded data),  
- has a clear pilot metric (retention, savings rate, reduced overdraft, fewer late fees, engagement),  
- fits NEXT’s workshop/community model,  
- uses AI in a “non-obvious” way (simulation, detection, coaching loops, content personalization), not only Q&A.

| High | **Micro-simulator for life choices** | NEXT community likely discusses moving out, cars, travel; strong engagement hook | AI builds scenario distributions, making uncertainty explicit (not just a single number) | MVP: scenario templates + user data; outputs best/base/worst and “break-even” rules |
| Medium-high | **Fraud & Scam Gym** | EBA highlights fraud and AI-enabled scams; fits “education that protects” | AI generates adaptive simulations and tracks skill improvement | MVP: 10 scenario generator + scoring; run in NEXT workshops |
| Medium | **Auto sinking funds engine** | Converts “unexpected expenses” into planned micro-actions; helps emergency savings | AI learns irregular expense patterns and builds a smoothing plan | MVP: detect annual/quarterly expenses; recommend monthly set-aside + autopay reminders |
| Medium | **Ask the Advisor prep** | Amplifies BTM’s relationship banking rather than replacing it | AI summarizes behavior and generates questions/trade-offs for human consult | MVP: upload data → one-page printable prep sheet for a branch meeting |

**Ideas deprioritized (for BTM-scale feasibility):**  
- Full “purchase overlay” integrations across merchants (complex distribution).  
- Emotion detection requiring deep behavioral data (privacy/compliance heavy).
